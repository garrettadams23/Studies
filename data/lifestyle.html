🏛️ Stoicism — The Art of Rational Endurance PHILOSOPHY ▶
Ancient Greek · Roman
Core Doctrines & Daily Practice
Founded ~300 BC by Zeno of Citium. Stoics held that virtue alone is sufficient for happiness and that emotions rooted in false judgements must be overcome through reason.
▸ THE FOUR CARDINAL VIRTUES
Wisdom (Sophia)
Knowing what is good, bad, and indifferent. The foundation of all other virtues.
Justice (Dikaiosyne)
Acting rightly toward others. Social duty; fairness in all dealings.
Courage (Andreia)
Facing hardship, pain, and death without flinching. Moral as well as physical.
Temperance (Sophrosyne)
Self-discipline and moderation. Mastery over appetites and impulses.
▸ CORE PRINCIPLES
Principle Meaning Practice
Dichotomy of Control Some things are "up to us" (prohairesis) — judgements, impulses, desires. Everything else is external and indifferent. Ask "Is this in my control?" before reacting to any event.
Memento Mori "Remember you will die." Mortality awareness as a tool for gratitude and urgency. Morning reflection: treat each day as a gift, not a given.
Amor Fati "Love of fate." Accept and embrace everything that happens — not mere tolerance, but affirmation. Reframe obstacles as opportunities; say "it was meant to be" and mean it.
Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Malorum) Deliberately imagine loss — of health, relationships, possessions — to appreciate what you have. Evening: briefly imagine losing what you value most, then feel gratitude for its presence.
Sympatheia All things are interconnected in one rational whole (the Logos). We are citizens of the universe. When frustrated by others: "They act from ignorance, not malice. I would do the same without better judgement."
▸ KEY FIGURES & TEXTS
Figure Era Key Work Contribution
Marcus Aurelius 121–180 AD Meditations Roman Emperor. Private journal of self-discipline; applied Stoicism in governance.
Epictetus 50–135 AD Enchiridion, Discourses Former slave. Focused on inner freedom regardless of external circumstance.
Seneca 4 BC–65 AD Letters to Lucilius, On the Shortness of Life Statesman and playwright. Practical moral essays on time, wealth, and death.
Zeno of Citium 334–262 BC — Founder of the Stoic school in Athens' painted porch (Stoa Poikile).
▸ DAILY STOIC PRACTICE
AM
Morning Journal
What obstacles might I face today? How will I respond virtuously? Rehearse the day — adversities and all.
MID
Pause & Label
When emotion arises, name it: "I notice anger." The gap between stimulus and response is where virtue lives.
PM
Evening Review
What did I do well? What could I have done better? No self-flagellation — just honest assessment. (Seneca's method)
♟️ Machiavellianism — Power, Realism & Strategy POLITICAL PHILOSOPHY ▶
Niccolò Machiavelli · 1469–1527
The Prince & the Discourses — Realpolitik Framework
Machiavelli separated political science from ethics. His work describes how power actually operates, not how it ideally should. Often misread as endorsing cruelty; actually a clinical observer of political reality.
▸ CORE CONCEPTS
Concept Meaning
Virtù Not "virtue" but prowess — strength, skill, cunning, willpower. The ruler's capacity to shape events through force of character and action.
Fortuna Fortune/chance — the unpredictable half of all outcomes. Virtù can master ~50% of fate; the rest is Fortuna. She favors the bold.
The Lion & the Fox A ruler must be both: the lion to frighten wolves (force), the fox to recognize traps (cunning). Neither alone is sufficient.
Economy of Violence Cruelties must be committed all at once; benefits distributed gradually. Sudden harshness is quickly forgotten; lingering harm festers.
Appearance vs. Reality It is unnecessary to actually possess virtues — it is sufficient to appear to have them. People judge by what they see, not what is.
Love vs. Fear It is safer to be feared than loved if you cannot be both. But avoid being hated — hatred is fatal; fear can be managed.
▸ THE 48 LAWS — ROBERT GREENE'S SYNTHESIS
Never Outshine the Master
Always make those above you feel superior. Conceal your talent when needed.
Conceal Your Intentions
Keep people off-balance. Reveal purpose only when it cannot be stopped.
Guard Your Reputation
Reputation is the cornerstone of power. A single crack and you are vulnerable.
Play on People's Need to Believe
People crave meaning. Offer a cause, a vision — they will follow willingly.
▸ DARK TRIAD CONTEXT (Psychology)
Trait Core Distinction
Machiavellianism Cynical worldview, strategic manipulation, delayed gratification Calculating and patient; manipulates for long-term gain
Narcissism Grandiosity, entitlement, need for admiration Emotional; manipulates for ego supply and status
Psychopathy Low empathy, impulsivity, callousness Reactive; least strategic of the three
🧠 Psychology — Mind, Behavior & Biases COGNITIVE SCIENCE ▶
Applied Psychology
Cognitive Biases · Defense Mechanisms · Frameworks
Psychology studies the mind and behavior. Understanding your cognitive architecture is the first step to overcoming it. "Knowing yourself is the beginning of all wisdom." — Aristotle
▸ KEY COGNITIVE BIASES
Bias Description Example
Confirmation Bias Seek info that confirms existing beliefs; ignore contradicting evidence Only reading news aligned with your politics
Dunning-Kruger Effect Incompetent people overestimate ability; experts underestimate theirs Beginner investor convinced they've outsmarted the market
Sunk Cost Fallacy Continue bad decisions because of past investment (time/money/effort) Finishing a terrible book because you've read half of it
Availability Heuristic Judge probability by how easily examples come to mind Fear flying after seeing news of a crash; ignoring car risk
Fundamental Attribution Error Over-attribute others' behavior to character; under-attribute to situation "They cut me off — they're a bad person" (vs. they're late for hospital)
Anchoring Over-rely on first piece of information encountered $100 item marked down to $70 feels like a deal regardless of value
Negativity Bias Negative events register more strongly than equally positive ones One criticism outweighs ten compliments
Halo Effect One positive trait causes assumption of other positive traits Attractive people assumed to be more competent
▸ EGO DEFENSE MECHANISMS (Freud / Anna Freud)
Mechanism Description
Repression Unconsciously pushing threatening thoughts out of awareness
Rationalization Creating logical explanations to justify unacceptable behavior
Projection Attributing your own unacceptable feelings to others
Denial Refusing to acknowledge a painful reality
Displacement Redirecting emotion from original source to safer target
Sublimation Channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activity (e.g., aggression → sport)
▸ KEY FRAMEWORKS
Maslow's Hierarchy
Physiological → Safety → Love/Belonging → Esteem → Self-Actualization. Lower needs must be satisfied before higher ones motivate.
Locus of Control
Internal: outcomes result from your actions. External: outcomes are luck/fate. Internal LoC correlates strongly with resilience and achievement.
Growth vs Fixed Mindset
Carol Dweck. Fixed: ability is innate and static. Growth: ability is developed through effort. Growth mindset predicts higher achievement and resilience.
Flow State (Csikszentmihalyi)
Optimal experience at the intersection of high challenge + high skill. Time distorts. Deep absorption. Intrinsic reward. Cultivated through deliberate practice.
📜 Philosophy — Schools of Thought WESTERN TRADITION ▶
Epistemology · Metaphysics · Ethics · Logic
Major Schools & Key Thinkers
▸ MAJOR SCHOOLS
School Era Core Claim Key Figures
Rationalism 17th–18th c. Knowledge derived primarily from reason, not senses. Innate ideas exist. Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz
Empiricism 17th–18th c. All knowledge derives from sensory experience. The mind starts as a blank slate (tabula rasa). Locke, Hume, Berkeley
Pragmatism 19th–20th c. Truth is what works in practice. Ideas are tools; their value is in their consequences. Peirce, James, Dewey
Existentialism 20th c. Existence precedes essence. Individuals create their own meaning in an indifferent universe. Sartre, Camus, Heidegger, de Beauvoir
Utilitarianism 18th–19th c. The morally right action maximizes overall happiness (utility) for the greatest number. Bentham, Mill
Kantian Ethics (Deontology) 18th c. Act only according to maxims you could will to be universal laws. Duty above consequences. Immanuel Kant
Virtue Ethics Ancient Greek Focus on developing good character (virtues) rather than rules or outcomes. Aristotle, MacIntyre
Nihilism 19th–20th c. Life has no intrinsic meaning, purpose, or value. Traditional morality is groundless. Nietzsche (critic), Cioran
▸ ESSENTIAL CONCEPTS
Concept Definition
Cogito ergo sum "I think, therefore I am." Descartes' foundational certainty — even a deceiver cannot doubt the existence of the doubter.
Categorical Imperative Kant: act only on maxims that could become universal laws. Treat humanity never merely as means but always as ends.
Absurdism Camus: life is inherently meaningless yet humans persistently seek meaning — this conflict is the Absurd. Response: revolt (not suicide, not faith).
Veil of Ignorance Rawls: design a just society without knowing your position in it. Ensures impartiality — you'd design fairness since you might be disadvantaged.
Übermensch Nietzsche's "Overman" — one who creates their own values after the "death of God." Not a racial concept; a call to self-overcoming.
Solipsism Only one's own mind is certain to exist. All else may be illusion. A logical extreme rarely endorsed but philosophically significant.
☸️ Buddhism — The Path to Liberation SPIRITUAL PHILOSOPHY ▶
Siddhartha Gautama · ~563–483 BC
Four Noble Truths · Eightfold Path · Core Concepts
Buddhism is both a religion and a philosophy. Its goal is the cessation of dukkha (suffering) through understanding the nature of mind and reality. Not theistic in the Western sense — the Buddha is a teacher, not a god.
▸ THE FOUR NOBLE TRUTHS (Catvāri Āryasatyāni)
1
Dukkha (Suffering)
Life inherently contains suffering, dissatisfaction, and imperfection. Birth, aging, sickness, death, getting what we don't want, not getting what we want — all are dukkha.
2
Samudāya (Origin)
The origin of suffering is craving (tanha) — for pleasure, for existence, for non-existence. Attachment and aversion are the roots.
3
Nirodha (Cessation)
Suffering can cease. Nirvana is the complete extinguishing of craving — a state of peace, freedom, and liberation.
4
Magga (The Path)
The Noble Eightfold Path is the way to end suffering. Practical and systematic — not metaphysical belief but lived practice.
▸ THE NOBLE EIGHTFOLD PATH
Path Category Meaning
Right View Wisdom Understanding the Four Noble Truths; seeing reality as it is, not as we wish it to be.
Right Intention Wisdom Commitment to non-attachment, non-ill-will, and non-harmfulness.
Right Speech Ethics Abstaining from lying, divisive speech, harsh speech, and idle chatter.
Right Action Ethics Abstaining from killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct.
Right Livelihood Ethics Earning a living that does not cause harm to others or oneself.
Right Effort Meditation Cultivating wholesome states and abandoning unwholesome ones continuously.
Right Mindfulness Meditation Present-moment awareness of body, feelings, mind-states, and phenomena.
Right Concentration Meditation Developing deep meditative absorption (jhanas) as foundation for insight.
▸ CORE CONCEPTS (THE THREE MARKS OF EXISTENCE)
Anicca — Impermanence
All conditioned phenomena are impermanent. Clinging to what is transient is the source of suffering. Release attachment.
Dukkha — Unsatisfactoriness
Even pleasant experiences carry an undercurrent of dissatisfaction because they end. Nothing conditioned can permanently satisfy.
Anatta — Non-Self
The "self" is not a fixed, permanent entity but a changing process of aggregates (skandhas). Liberating when truly understood.
☯️ Taoism — The Way of Nature CHINESE PHILOSOPHY ▶
Lao Tzu · ~6th–4th c. BC
Tao Te Ching — The Classic of the Way and Its Power
The Tao (道) — the Way — is the fundamental, unnamed principle underlying all of reality. It cannot be named or fully grasped, only lived. "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao."
▸ CORE PRINCIPLES
Principle Meaning Application
Wu Wei (無為) "Non-action" or effortless action. Acting in harmony with the natural flow rather than forcing outcomes. Avoid unnecessary struggle. Like water: yield, go around, persist gently.
Pu (樸) — Simplicity The uncarved block. Original nature before social conditioning. Simplicity as strength. Strip away pretense. Return to what is essential and natural.
Te (德) — Virtue/Power Inner virtue or power that arises naturally when aligned with the Tao. Not morality imposed from outside but integrity from within. Be genuinely good, not performatively good.
Yin-Yang (陰陽) All opposites are interdependent and contain each other's seed. Darkness within light, light within darkness. Change is constant. Stop seeing circumstances as purely good or bad. Both are transforming.
Ziran (自然) Self-so, naturalness. Things should be allowed to follow their own nature. Stop trying to control what is inherently wild and self-organizing.
▸ WATER AS TAOIST METAPHOR
Water is the supreme Taoist metaphor. It is:
· Soft yet wears down the hardest rock (persistence)
· Low — it always seeks the lowest point (humility)
· Yielding — flows around obstacles rather than fighting them
· Essential — all life depends on it, yet it does not demand recognition
"Nothing in the world is as soft and yielding as water, yet nothing is better at dissolving the hard and inflexible."
🌑 Existentialism & Absurdism 20TH C. PHILOSOPHY ▶
Sartre · Camus · Kierkegaard · Heidegger
"Existence precedes essence" — Building meaning in a meaningless universe
Existentialism holds that there is no pre-given human nature or purpose. We exist first, then create ourselves through choices. This is both terrifying (radical freedom) and liberating.
▸ KEY CONCEPTS
Concept Thinker Meaning
Radical Freedom Sartre We are "condemned to be free" — there is no escape from choice. Even refusing to choose is a choice. Total responsibility for self.
Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi) Sartre Pretending you have no choice; adopting a fixed identity to escape freedom. E.g., "I can't help it, that's just who I am."
The Absurd Camus The conflict between humans' need for meaning and the universe's silent indifference. Three responses: physical suicide (wrong), philosophical suicide (religion — bad faith), or revolt (the only authentic response).
Authenticity Heidegger Owning your existence — facing death (Being-toward-death) rather than fleeing into das Man (the "they-self" of conformity).
Leap of Faith Kierkegaard Rational thought cannot resolve the deepest questions. Authentic existence requires a passionate, subjective commitment despite uncertainty.
Thrownness (Geworfenheit) Heidegger We are "thrown" into existence — we did not choose our time, place, language, culture. But we are responsible for what we do with this situation.
▸ THE MYTH OF SISYPHUS — CAMUS
Sisyphus is condemned to roll a boulder up a hill for eternity — it always rolls back down. Camus argues this is the human condition.

The resolution: "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
Not because the task is meaningful in any cosmic sense, but because the struggle itself fills his heart. Revolt against absurdity — continuing to live, create, love — is the only authentic response.
⬜ Minimalism & Intentional Living LIFESTYLE ▶
Modern Practice
Intentionality · Essentialism · Deep Work
▸ CORE PRINCIPLES
Essentialism (Greg McKeown)
The disciplined pursuit of less. Not about doing more things — about doing the right things. "If it's not a clear yes, it's a no."
Deep Work (Cal Newport)
Cognitively demanding tasks performed in a state of distraction-free concentration. The skill of the 21st century. Rare + valuable = leverage.
KonMari Method
Keep only what "sparks joy." Tidy by category, not location. The goal: create an environment that supports who you want to become.
Parkinson's Law
Work expands to fill the time allotted. Impose artificial constraints. Deadlines create focus. Less time + clear priority = better output.
▸ HIGH-PERFORMANCE LIFESTYLE FRAMEWORKS
Framework Key Idea Source
80/20 Principle (Pareto) 80% of results come from 20% of efforts. Identify and double down on the vital few. Ruthlessly eliminate the trivial many. Vilfredo Pareto / Tim Ferriss
Atomic Habits Systems over goals. 1% better every day = 37x better in a year. Identity-based habits: "I am a writer" vs "I want to write." James Clear
Ikigai Japanese: reason for being. The intersection of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. Japanese philosophy
Memento Vivere "Remember to live." The counterpoint to Memento Mori — not just contemplating death, but using that awareness to fully inhabit life. Stoic tradition
Wabi-Sabi Japanese aesthetic: find beauty in imperfection, transience, and incompleteness. A cracked bowl repaired with gold (Kintsugi) is more beautiful for its history. Japanese philosophy
🌙 Wicca — Sabbats, Elements & Tools PRACTICE GUIDE ▶
Tradition
Wicca — Modern Witchcraft Tradition
Wicca is a modern Pagan religion codified by Gerald Gardner (1954) and further shaped by Doreen Valiente. It centres on reverence for nature, polarity of Goddess and God, seasonal cycles, and magical practice.

Wiccan Rede: "An it harm none, do what ye will."  ·  Rule of Three: Energy sent returns threefold.

☀️ Paganism — Traditions & Core Concepts OVERVIEW ▶
Overview
Contemporary Paganism — Nature-Based Spirituality
Paganism is a broad category of earth-centred, polytheistic, or animistic spiritual traditions. Common themes: reverence for nature, cyclical time, pre-Christian deities or archetypes, and personal spiritual sovereignty. No single holy text or central authority governs all paths.

Hellenism Ancient Greek Olympian pantheon, philosophy, polis religion
Kemetism Ancient Egyptian Ma'at, Osiris, Isis, ritual purity, heka magic
Celticism Celtic cultures Tuatha Dé Danann, sacred sites, seasonal festivals

▸ CORE CONCEPTS

Polytheism Multiple distinct deities; hard poly = literal gods; soft poly = archetypes
Immanence The divine is present within nature and the material world
Sacred Calendar Ritual life aligned with solar (Sabbats) and lunar (Esbats) cycles

🌳 Druidism — Grades, Awen & Sacred Concepts PRACTICE GUIDE ▶
Tradition
Druidry — Celtic Spiritual Path
Druidry draws on Celtic heritage, nature mysticism, and Bardic oral tradition. Modern revival began in the 18th century. Major orders: OBOD (Order of Bards, Ovates and Druids, est. 1964) and ADF (Ár nDraíocht Féin, est. 1983). Awen (Welsh: /ˈaʊ.ɛn/) — divine inspiration flowing through all things; symbolised by three rays of light ☀.
▸ THREE GRADES
🎵
Bard
First grade. Keeper of memory, story, and song. Develops creative voice, poetry, and the craft of sacred expression. Studies Celtic mythology & lore.
🌿
Ovate
Second grade. Seer, healer, and naturalist. Works with divination (Ogham, scrying), herbalism, communion with ancestors and the spirit world.
🌳
Druid
Third grade. Philosopher, ritualist, and mediator. Deep study of cosmology, ceremony, law & ethics. Bridge between the human and divine.
